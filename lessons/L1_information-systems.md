## What is an Information System?

> A set of interrelated components that **collect, process, store, and distribute** information to support decision-making, coordination, control, analysis, and visualisation within an organisation.

---

## IS Process: Data → Information

**Information** is data shaped into meaningful form — a representation of reality.

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Input** | Captures raw data from the organisation or external environment |
| **Processing** | Converts raw data into meaningful form |
| **Output** | Transfers processed information to people or activities that use it |
| **Feedback** | Output is returned to appropriate members to evaluate or correct the input stage |

---

## Core Dimensions of an IS

All three dimensions are equally critical and converge at the IS:

| Dimension | Focus |
|-----------|-------|
| **Technology** | Hardware, software, networks, databases |
| **Organisation** | Structure, culture, processes, people |
| **Management** | Leadership, strategy, decision-making |

---

## Key Stakeholders

The IS operates within a broader environment interacting with:

| Stakeholder Group | Relationship |
|-------------------|-------------|
| Customers & Suppliers | Business relationships |
| Stockholders & Regulatory agencies | Governance / compliance |
| Competitors | Market dynamics |

---

## Business–IS Interdependence

Growing interdependence between:
- Ability to **use information technology**
- Ability to **implement corporate strategies** and achieve goals

### Modern Business Challenges
- Need for real-time decisions
- Demand for digital transformation
- Competitive pressure to innovate
- Required system flexibility

### IS Shapes Business Capabilities
- Business strategies depend on IT capabilities
- Changes in business require changes in IT
- IT limitations can restrict business options
- IT innovations enable new business opportunities for all stakeholders

---

## Complementary Assets

Technology alone is not enough. Success depends on complementary investments:
- Supportive **organisational culture**
- **Staff training** to boost adoption and reduce resistance
- Streamlined **processes** that maximise the value of new technologies

> *Example: A hospital's CSSD adopted RFID for inventory tracking — success was driven by process reengineering and targeted staff training, not the technology alone.*

## Academic Disciplines in IS Study

### Technical Approach
| Discipline | Focus |
|------------|-------|
| Computer Science | Hardware, software, networks, databases |
| Management Science | Process modelling, analysis, optimisation |

### Behavioural Approach
| Discipline | Focus |
|------------|-------|
| Sociology | Group dynamics, organisational impact, social structures |
| Psychology | User perception, decision-making, system usage |
| Economics | Digital goods, market dynamics, cost structures |
| Political Science | Power relations and control mechanisms |

### Sociotechnical View
- Integration of social + technical systems
- Helps avoid a purely technological approach
- IS must be jointly optimised across both dimensions

### Supporting Fields
| Field | Contribution |
|-------|-------------|
| Operations Research | Process optimisation, resource allocation |
| Legal Studies | Privacy, intellectual property, liability |

---

## Key Takeaways

1. An IS transforms raw **data → information** through Input → Processing → Output → Feedback.
2. IS has three equally critical dimensions: **Technology, Organisation, Management**.
3. IS does not operate in isolation — it interacts with external **stakeholders** (customers, suppliers, regulators, competitors).
4. **Business and IS are interdependent** — strategy shapes IT needs, and IT capabilities shape business strategy.
5. Technology investment requires **complementary assets** (culture, training, process) to deliver value.
6. IS is studied through **technical, behavioural, and sociotechnical** lenses.
